{{% callout emoji="💡" %}} This is the third and final entry of [a series of introspective posts](/t/reflection-age-legacy/) intended as a primarily therapeutic endeavor. Given the intent to initially provide one cohesive article, these subsequent entries are continuations of thought in the previous entries.{{% /callout %}}

While I’ve covered the broader changes this past year has brought into my life and provided context to the previous 10 years to explain how it is relatively dramatic for someone with a [neurodevelopmental disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder), I have glossed over many struggles and hardships I’ve faced. This includes many that exist today, whether pre-existing or otherwise, as I’ve mentioned in the first entry. Some might be more relevant to this reflection, but I wish to set a boundary for things I air out to the world. Not to deny them of their real influence nor to be secretive about my struggles—many of which I have already spoken about publicly, at least to some who would be reading this—but I have another reason…

…you see, I am not a person who is great at venting, at least about the more profound, hard-hitting subjects. I’m great at venting for the small things without significance to anyone. However, I struggle to be open with the categories of issues I would tell others they should be comfortable venting about to find healing (another point to this “ironic hypocrisy” of mine). This isn’t to say that I am ashamed of these things or that I don’t wish to acknowledge the mere thought of their existence; many will know that I’m an open book with these. What I struggle with, which I believe is a more recent development from the struggles of the past *few* years, is the thought that by airing them out directly, in my filterless thoughts, I am bound to commit sins against others and myself.

Maybe that seems like a non sequitur, and perhaps that’s true. Still, I feel as though I’ve had one too many situations where I will vent about a certain situation or have multiple ventings to someone over a while about common denominators (I mean people, mainly), only to feed them seemingly this characterization of someone drawn solely by my feelings with particular events. This isn’t a wholly irrational thought either; I have witnessed this amongst some friends about specific people. I feel it’s a combination of multiple factors:

1. I don’t talk to people where I’m giving gratitude or praise for another person, say if person *x* does something nice for whoever or if person *y* is valuable to me. It’s not how I’ve operated; I keep these thoughts to myself and sometimes the person I’m thankful for. This means that when I vent to someone about a situation involving someone, it’s one of the few times I talk about that person besides other more benign moments of relevance.
2. I speak directly and carefully, being transparent with my position and as objective as possible concerning my feelings and perceptions. Maybe it’s a delusional thought, but I have this notion that people tend to see me as a pretty objective person, and so the things I say carry a more authoritative voice with them.
3. Out of the goodwill of those trusted figures, they sympathize with my situation(s) and validate my feelings toward them. Still, many have not approached with the rationality required—the acknowledgment that what I share is ultimately only one side of the story. This isn’t to say that I lie or exaggerate details in these situations, but when combined with point 1, it results in an incomplete picture of certain people and situations.

To someone more attentive, this probably reads as one elaborate way to form a mental trap, and I won’t be the one to deny them that interpretation. Regardless, it is what I’ve “learned” (a la coping mechanism) due to many difficult situations. I won’t excuse myself and say that this is the correct way to go about it or that what I do to this end is anything good or healthy. Still, it is the way that (I’m convinced) I avoid making false characterizations, even if not entirely by my hand. I, too, understand that this doesn’t fall entirely on me; it requires the critical thinking of those I’ve entrusted not to see it as the whole picture, no matter how clear I am about the particular situations—but I’ve been fooled twice.

### How do I reframe myself around these new defining factors I face?

A boon to all this would be finding ways to address my aforementioned venting crux, but that isn’t currently on the table for me. As much as I would like to have an outlet to speak on many of these situations freely, the reality is that I cannot convince myself that doing so amounts to much good when it seems to be at the expense of others’ reputations.

To be plain, I’ve primarily approached these things with a “so be it” type of attitude, with some disdain and lament sprinkled throughout. I don’t believe it’s the most healthy option, but I see it as more sacrificial. Sacrifice is the nature of carrying one’s cross. With many of the hard pills I’ve had to swallow in these past years, this feels in some sense natural and logical.

I’m willing to accept that I’ve developed into a new wave of depression, expressing itself in substantially different ways than I’ve known, whether firsthand or secondhand. I’m willing to accept it is something more significant than that, given all of the changes in thought patterns and coping mechanisms to justify what happens to me and why I handle it the way I do, even with all the caveats. I’m very good at internalizing, but maybe for once in a very long time, I’ve begun to internalize the things I shouldn’t have.

So, like with many of the questions I’ve asked myself in this reflection, the answer is, once again, “I don’t know.” I’m a person who analyzes a problem the moment it arrives to find a solution efficiently and put it into action, meaning that if I had an answer or even an inclination, I would have already sought to apply it and hopefully move on. This hasn’t happened with many of these situations; in some, I’ve gone a path that may have been best avoided altogether.

---

### Conclusion

The conclusion here is that, ultimately, I am a broken being. This isn’t anything new or necessarily unique, but this reflection allows me to shed some light on where I am and what I’m dealing with, to what end beyond opening up some I don’t know. These struggles often lead me to a state where I can’t even begin to think about God despite Him being the only one who can put me back together. Please take an opportunity to pray for me and those around me who have to deal more directly with the Seraphim who carries the weight of all these things, the Seraphim whose ADHD remarkably won’t let him forget about it, unlike most other things. Thank you for reading.
