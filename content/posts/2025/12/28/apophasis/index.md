> "What, therefore, do I say and write? That nothing, Olympia, is so worthy of consideration as patience in the midst of pain and grief. For this is the queen of good things, the perfection of crowns, and just as it surpasses other virtues, so this particular form of virtue especially surpasses the others in brilliance."
> 
> St. John Chrysostom, Letters to Olympia

Dysthymia is a cross I bear. It informs much of my perceptions, how I view the world, how I relate to others, how I observe (or fail to) the forests of life over the individual trees. More abstractly, it is a part of who I am to the extent that removing all of its impact to my psyche would be much like taking a greyscale photo of a rainbow, despite how very greyscale I often feel.

It is no joy to have a personality developed through a complex web of apophatic refinements, to be largely defined by the things I've learned not to do as a result of various difficult situations or observing the destructive patterns of those in my upbringing. After all, defense/coping mechanisms are not rewards, they fail to produce even the slightest amount of stimulation that could otherwise be motivating given the ADHD and all that. It is not triumph, I neither conquered nor overcame anything. I endured much, but simply enduring feels only worthy of participation awards[^1], writing "I was here" on the walls of wherever it happened would've been more impactful. I *was* there, yet I became something else after the fact.

---

Digressing some, I find myself at an interesting stage in my spiritual life, especially with the Nativity feast having just begun. I've lost much of the joy, even much of the foolish contentment I had[^2] in spiritual things. The festivities, and the very nature of the feast, make this observation all the more apparent.

For better or worse (I'd like to think "for better"), this has grew slowly over my years of being an Orthodox Christian. Despite the lingering skeleton in the closet, I managed to dupe myself into pretending that it simply isn't there. I'd like to think I succeeded in that quite a bit, enough to put myself forward to become minor order clergy even.

To be clear, I don't blame the faith for the state that I'm in. I do not necessarily view some common perspectives within Orthodoxy to be helpful to this end, but these deficiencies only exist to the extent that humans exist in the Church. I have no uncertainty that there are folks in other traditions that carry these same sorts of weights, especially those who also struggle with dysthymia.

---

I find that a lived experience of Orthodoxy carries a spectrum about how someone pursues Christ, on one end lies fear, the other love. You could articulate this in many other ways, for example "being Christian out of fear of condemnation" or "being Christian out of love for Christ." There are certain factors to one's spiritual life that might presuppose one's place on this spectrum. For example, a convert might lean more on the side of fear[^3], while someone who has a developed, experience-rich faith leans more on the side of love[^4]. Similarly, I find that men have a tendency to lean in the direction of fear, while women tend to lean in the direction of love.

Unlike many spectrums that might exist in the context of an Orthodox Christian, this is not one where a balance is necessary. As St. John says, fear is nonexistent when one's love is perfect. There is no "wrong" place on this spectrum either - the worst that this spectrum could articulate about someone is spiritual immaturity, but how many of us would be so bold to say they're spiritually mature anyway?

The church fathers, including those of recent memory, demonstrate this spectrum. On one end you may find someone like St. Joseph the Hesychast who speaks more about the struggle against sin directly, the other you have St. Porphyrios of Kavsokalyvia who emphasizes an "easier path" found in focusing on love for Christ and others. Neither of these are invalid paths and one size does not fit all. Many of us often need a bit of both to continue growing toward that perfect love.

Nevertheless, this sort of duality is not the whole of it. There is, in some sense, a secret third category: not existing on it at all.

By no means is this a good or normal thing, of course, but it is the place I find myself with this state that has been growing over the past few years. I have no tangible perfection in my love, nor do I carry much fear for condemnation[^5]. One could even say this secret third option lies in the depths of being at the perfect center of the spectrum. It's something I would label as something like "mindful inertia", where I am affirming of the faith and what it holds (and explain/teach it to others) yet remain stiff in practice, from the aforementioned dysthymia and struggles with executive dysfunction[^6]. While I may find myself hopeless, I still have hope, if only in subtlety and mere intellect.

A compounding factor is that the path ahead in this context isn't clear. On one hand, I feel I need a sort of "re-evangelizing" - a new, refreshing encounter with Christ. On the other, I could've been given this already (numerous times, even) and invalidated it through my despondency and listlessness. My dispositions lead me to remain inactive because activity does not appear to lead to anything, I possess no real intrinsic reinforcement toward spiritual pursuits. I perceive no feedback in my efforts, so I am mentally led to observe the seemingly inconclusive result. Given the amount of times this has happened, leading up until now, it appears unreasonable to do the same thing again to expect a different result.

The passions that afflict me are ones that I've largely concluded (through spiritual counsel, etc.) will not leave me, short of direct intervention from God. While, yes, I have a responsibility in resisting them nonetheless, it's like constantly watering plants that have died decades ago. It doesn't appear to change anything, yet it's what I'm told I have to do, that one day something might be different.

I find some meager hope in saints like St. Silouan the Athonite and his spiritual progeny, given their somewhat existentialist approach to these same sorts of dilemmas, yet they don't succumb to the temptation to just forget everything and move onto something else. They keep (and kept) their minds in hell, and despair not[^7]. But what is one to do if they've acquiesced to that despair?

St. Sophrony of Essex would say to forget about that and pour all of your troubles onto God anyway[^8], that despite all of the spiritual ailment, God is still here and welcomes us back. We just have to admit it and forego even the false pride in assuming that the conclusions we make from our (somewhat true) observations are false. Maybe that is what I need, but maybe I end up right back where I am now, because while I can do this, there is no guarantee that I receive what I want out of the exchange. Will I receive what I need? Intellectually speaking, yes, this is God's promise. But what I feel I need and what I actually need are not the same, and how much more disappointment can I bear?

I realize that many of my writings here seem to conclude with an unclear dead end, unfortunately this one is not going to be different. I find myself in another situation where it's as though my soul is in a cryogenic state, waiting for whatever discovery is necessary to be unfrozen and alive once more. I long for the times when I was not locked in this mental fortress of despair and I feel as though my intentions ought to be enough to get a helping hand, but that doesn't appear to be realistic. Still, how do I find the boldness to take the kingdom of heaven by force[^9] and turn away from the hedonism that offers a mirage of stability? Time will only tell, I suppose.

Please keep me in your prayers.
