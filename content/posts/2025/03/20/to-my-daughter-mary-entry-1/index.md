You've made it — one whole revolution around the sun.

The day you were born, winter that year began its last hurrah in the form of a relatively unimpactful but highly visible snowfall[^1]. One year later, while the ground is much less snow-covered than last year, we had a similar snow all through the morning and early afternoon. I don't consider myself the type to be too invested in these sorts of coincidences, but I can't help but be captivated and charmed by their presence.

Before you, life moved rather quickly, possibly *too* quickly. Yet, your existence has brought about a calm and grounded experience of life[^2]. The foresightedness demanded of parents has all but required us to take things little-by-little, from the first night home. In some weird sort of tension, this year has taught us that time is not our own and we have to exercise much patience, but also that life won't wait.

---

The first couple months of parenthood were rough, solely from the lifestyle adjustments you asked of us. Nonetheless, you were patient with us, and your dispositions were nothing short of soothing for two people in their mid-late 20s trying to figure all this out. The following couple months were much better, you started making cute squeaks and smiling. We eventually worked out that you find someone pretending to eat your tummy the pinnacle of humor, cackling like a little goblin.[^3]

Your third month of life was a sprint in development for you, your personality was beginning to emerge: your sociable nature, your loquacity, and your expressiveness. You went from a machine that turns milk to poop, requiring lots of recharging throughout the day, to an ambitious little human, ready to take on all the novelty in life. In your fourth month, the cogs in your brain started getting it's groove—you started figuring out toys. It was this month that we brought home Barsanuphius[^4], your feline partner-in-crime that you weren't really certain of at first, but now it's evident that you two operate on very similar wavelengths (although you are much less of a gluttonous menace than him). He was nearly three-months-old when we adopted him and has been remarkably tolerant to the grief you've subjected him to over the months.

You also discovered watermelon, which you thought was the coolest food to ever exist until the introduction of meat much time later. Regardless, it was this month and the following one that you started eating more proper food. While your food preferences were pretty up-in-the-air, it was certain that you enjoyed this newfangled thing called "eating food."

Ages five to seven months, not much changed, but two things were made apparent: you love to talk, but you **hate** raspberries. Your eighth month of life you were able to say "mama" and "dada" pretty proficiently ("ma" was your favorite syllable of the year by far). You experienced snow proper and became an even greater social butterfly than you already were. But a couple months later, you discovered locomotion and started crawling—just in time for your nana, grandpapa, and auntie's arrival for Christmas—but you decided to put some words on hold for a bit, I wouldn't hear "dada" for quite some time. Your first Nativity was wonderful and you loved all the toys and goodies your relatives from near and afar brought you, but a couple weeks later, we'd all go through a pretty sizable rollercoaster...

..you caught COVID *and* RSV. At first encounter, it didn't seem like it would have been much of a problem, but then we learned that when you have a fever, you just aren't up to doing much eating and drinking. Unfortunately, this meant you were hospitalized. You were there for a couple days, although the larger portion of it was in the emergency room. In the grand scheme of everything, this was merely a blip, but I'd be lying if I said mom and I were in our best states of being, having to bear witness to it all[^5]. It was arguably the hardest challenge of your first year: watching you endure it all, from the IV hookup to the pains of your restlessness and hunger.

Once it was over, it was like it never happened. You went back to your ol' goofy, expressive self, but this time you made quite a comeback in saying "dada". The next two months flew by, but not long before you turned one, you started saying your first two-worded sentence: "hi dada."

---

Life has been so drastically different since you came into our lives. I can't speak for your mother, but it was a lot of growing up in a short amount of time—having to resist the temptations to live life as I had before you were a responsibility, with the most impulse-denial I've had to do in my ADHD-ridden life.

None of that is a complaint. You've helped me become a better man and I could not be more thankful that I get to have you, my little cheerleader. I know the early mornings when you wake me up ready to take on the day might not give that impression, but know that I wouldn't change it for the world. You're amazing.

There's a lot ahead of us, from potential siblings to new, unexplored territories—not to mention walking and talking soon, Lord willing. But whatever happens, I know that I've got the happiest little goob by my side.

I don't know if you'll ever read this. It'd certainly be a long time until you do, and heavily dependent on whether I keep up this whole self-archivist nature of things. But if I do, just remember this: the world will try to take your happiness and peace from you, but resist every moment of it. It is your happiness and peace that can save thousands around you. You've provided the brightest light in my life and I have no doubt that others have and will witness it too. Let your light shine, and don't let anyone or anything dim you out.

I love you.

Dada (but in rare occasions, "Daddy")
