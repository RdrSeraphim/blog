Christianity here in the United States is significantly different from that of other countries. In fact, it has largely informed much of the major ideological shifts the U.S. has had, including before the U.S. was properly a country. Despite how apparent this contrast is, it is not in the common consciousness of how this came to be. I reckon generations prior to my own could certainly point to the rise of the Christian right as a distinct political bloc in the 1970s as a contributing factor. Otherwise, there is relative silence on the "how" and "why", and ultimately the "why" of how Christianity continues to decline by ~5% and secularism continues to grow by ~10% every 5 years[[1]](#fn1).

I've been fascinated by this development for a while given that it didn't come out of nowhere, yet is so unknown without doing a good amount of digging around. As such, this article will take on three hats, in hopes of cohesively articulating the religious landscape the U.S. finds itself in.

1. The first three Great Awakenings and their significance to American culture and politics.
2. The Fourth Great Awakening
3. Modern contributors to the continued divide.

### The Awakenings before 1960

The first and second Great Awakenings are the most documented of the three before 1960.

#### The First Great Awakening

Historian Bernard Bailyn described the First Great Awakening as "the central event in the history of religion in America in the eighteenth century[[2]](#fn2)." It's generally agreed upon that the First Great Awakening coincided with the evangelization campaigns of the "Great Itinerant" co-founder of Methodism, George Whitefield, beginning in the late 1730s. Not long before this was the revival efforts of Jonathan Edwards (of "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God" fame, published in 1741) starting in 1734, but waning by 1738 - the same year that George Whitefield began his campaign in Georgia[[3]](#fn3).

The First Great Awakening largely focused on personal evangelism and the particularities of the extant revivalism by Whitefield, Edwards, and others. Namely, that religious conversion was less an intellectual endeavor but rather a more profound, emotional, transcending "rebirth" (or being "born again", if you will) - where rote doctrine became less important than outpourings of the Holy Spirit[[4]](#fn4). One might see glimpses of Pentecostalism in this, and you would be right. However, Pentecostalism still needed another 150 years to gestate, so we'll revisit the denomination later.

It is worth noting that revivals and awakenings are not the same thing, despite the two having some interplay. Revivals are isolated events of individual conversion, whereas awakenings are spans of cultural revitalization[[5]](#fn5).

Along with this came great challenges toward the clerical establishment of what was mostly New England, where revivalists accused many of the "orthodox", "unconverted" (i.e. not sensationalist; smells like donatism in here) clergy of corrupting the church through lust of power and material luxury[[6]](#fn6), creating enough kerfuffle to have laws quickly written against them and their practices resulting in numerous arrests for various revivalists in the 1740s and 50s[[7]](#fn7).
