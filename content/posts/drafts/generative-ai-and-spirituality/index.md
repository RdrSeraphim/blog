I think AI's neat. There's a lot of great and wholesome potential in it. It can help summarize and organize thoughts, create fun images of absolutely bombastic things (like [Santa Claus arm-wrestling Jimi Hendrix on the Golden Gate Bridge in front of a crowd of Bluey characters](/posts/images/2025/06/095743a3-a90a-4abf-8a8a-047b09af7ab7.png)), and quickly respond to various problems otherwise trivial to a computer. For my generation, it's like WolframAlpha but for way more than math and statistics.

With every sort of new technology comes a hypetrain, and with hypetrains come die-hard loyalists who proclaim the good news of the technology while being ignorant of the traps lying within. AI is the latest craze and will likely remain so until something more immediately economically stimulating comes along. Before this, it was cryptocurrency and NFTs. All it takes is for the right person knowledgeable in large language models and an intrigue for Christianity to fill a newly established vacuum.

Even in the short amount of time that AI has been accessible to the masses, we've seen adoption even among more serious pieces of software pertaining to the faith. The Logos Bible Software, a tool for extensive Bible study, homily creation, etc., [has AI-powered functions for researching and writing](https://support.logos.com/hc/en-us/articles/30426521209869-How-to-use-AI-Tools-for-Research-and-Writing). There are at least two other AIs you can easily find straight off Google, like "[Bible AI](https://www.bibleai.com/)" and "[BibleGPT](https://biblegpt-la.com/)". This isn't even accounting for prompts you could give to something like ChatGPT to give it a role of spiritual director, liturgist, or what have you. 

However, none of this takes into account the reality of AI. The immaturity, the lack of objectivity, the more hallucinatory underbelly. AI
